INTERNSHIPS

SERVICE LEARNING INTERNSHIPS, 1-3 UNITS, (P/NP)

Internships through The Transfer Center are developed each semester and focus on addressing community needs, fostering leadership, and public service. Students can earn 1-3 units of academic credit while they take part in serving the needs of a dynamic student community.

In collaboration with staff, interns develop a specific service learning project area, with the goal of enhancing and/or expanding campus and community resources and enriching the university experience for transfer students.

A commitment of 45 hours per semester is required for each unit of academic credit available through the School of Social Welfare.

1 unit of credit = 45 semester hours
2 units of credit = 90 semester hours
3 units of credit = 135 semester hours

GRADING PROCEDURE:

In order to receive a passing grade students must:

  • Complete the required number of service hours based on unit enrollment,
  • Submit a monthly internship timesheet to their project supervisor for review,
  • Meet regularly with an assigned project supervisor for on-going information, training and/or to discuss project implementation.
  • Participate in required training/orientation activities
  • Document the final completion/outcomes of the service learning project.

STUDENT REQUIREMENTS:

Students will be required to:

  • Meet weekly with an assigned supervisor to discuss the accomplishment of specific learning objectives,
  • Become familiar with the centers programs, policies and procedures,
  • Develop an overview of community resources and campus services,
  • Explore needs and issues relevant to a culturally diverse student population.

PEER ADVOCATES

  • This team of transfer students staff the information and referral desk during regular hours of operation, Monday-Friday,9 am-5 pm. They serve as key resources on navigating the academic, social and cultural terrain f the university. They provide additional assistance by helping to keep the center a welcoming and inviting space for all transfer students on campus.

Here is a short interview with Steven Nguyen, The Transfer Student Center's Academic Achievement Counselor, answering some questions on the roles of Peer Advocates and Interns in the Transfer Student Center:

Q: What are some qualities you look for when hiring a Peer Advocate/selecting an intern?
A: I usually seek out individual who show Initiative, Customer Service Skills, Public Speaking skills, and have interest in helping others.

Q: What is the difference between and intern and a Peer Advocate?
A: Interns and Peer Advocate do not differ too much. Interns get units, Peer Advocate get compensated.

Q: How do you train your students?
A: We have a Formal Training Schedule.

Q: How do you create a weekly schedule?
A: We use a Doodle to see everyones availability. I then create a calendar for the week, send it out to all Peer Advocates/Interns and post it at the front desk.

Q: What is expected of a Peer Advocate?
A: They must work 10 hours, represent the transfer center, and participate in project areas.

Q: How do you feel the Peer Advocates and Interns benefit the center?
A: This is outlined in the Transfer Student Center Peer Advocate Manual . They are the life and heart beat of the center. They truly are the center

Q: What is your job, as a leader, for these student employees?
A: To help inform them to the best of my knowledge and create a environment they enjoying working for.

The Lead Peer Advocate

1. What are some qualities you think are important for a Lead Peer Advocate to have?

  • A Lead Peer Advocate should have experience, initiative, drive/passion, and presence. A lead peer advocate should be a peer advocate before transitioning into the lead role in order to gain the knowledge of the transfer resources, the transfer culture, and the job itself. While working as a peer advocate, the lead should show initiative in "bettering" the transfer student center, such as finding ways to improve transfer resources, provide better service for the students that enter the center, improving the transfer student space, etc. This initiative will also reflect on his/her passion and drive for the work done at the center. Like any good leader, the lead peer advocate should have good social/communication skills that will allow for him/her to be successful in guiding other peer advocates as well as the transfer students on campus. Lastly, his/her presence will allow for others to also be passionate for the mission of the transfer student center.

2. What sets you apart from Peer Advocates?

  • The Lead Peer Advocate typically has shown a strong sense of leadership within the role. The lead shows a strong work ethic and organizational skills that allow for exemplary leadership on and off the job. The lead's initiative to improve the center allows for him/her to stand out from the rest of the peer advocates.

3. What are your most important responsibilities and how do you handle them?

  • The Lead Peer Advocate organizes and produces the weekly front desk schedule, while communicating with the peer advocates to fill in any and all empty shifts. The Lead Peer Advocate takes a leadership role in several events that require a Transfer representative. The lead also helps plan and facilitate the training sessions for the peer advocates and any other meetings with the transfer staff. The lead should be able to assist the peer advocates with any questions, concerns, or activities, as well as oversee the needs at the front desk.

4. Is there anything in particular that you would like to share with a future Lead?

  • "Have patience with the different roles that you have to play while being a Lead. The job requires for you to get creative sometimes so don't be afraid of trying new methods to achieve your duties as a Lead. Never feel like you can't be unsure about something. Ask many questions! Keep learning and challenging yourself. Enjoy what you do and always let your passion flow with the work that you do at the center."
